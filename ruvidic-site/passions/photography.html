Macro Photography

Macro photography is about slowing down. Finding a subject — an insect on a flower, water droplets on a leaf, the texture of bark — and then spending the time to get the light, the angle, and the focus exactly right. The margin for error is zero. At 1:1 magnification, the depth of field is measured in millimetres.

Equipment: Sony A7 IV with Sony 90mm f/2.8 G Macro OSS and Irix 150mm f/2.8 Dragonfly Macro. Both lenses reward patience — they are sharp tools for a slow discipline.

Astrophotography

Astrophotography is the opposite of macro in scale — but identical in discipline. You are chasing faint light that has travelled thousands of light years. The Orion Nebula, the Horsehead, the Andromeda galaxy. Objects that require dark skies, precise polar alignment, and hours of exposure stacking.

The process is as much about data processing as it is about capture. Stacking dozens of sub-exposures, calibrating with darks and flats, stretching the histogram carefully to reveal structure without destroying the background — it is methodical work that rewards precision.

Approach Both disciplines share the same core requirement: you cannot rush them. A macro shot taken in haste is blurry. An astrophoto rushed through processing loses detail. The best results come from taking the time to do it properly — which is also, not coincidentally, how good security work gets done.
