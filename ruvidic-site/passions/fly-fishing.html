The River as
Meditation

Fly fishing is not just fishing — it's reading water, knowing entomology, the precision of a cast, and the deep silence alongside a river.

Why Fly Fishing

Most fishing is about waiting. Fly fishing is about thinking. Every decision matters: which fly, what size, how to present it, where exactly to cast. The fish live in specific lies — predictable positions in the current where food arrives with minimal effort. Finding those lies, and presenting a fly that matches what the fish are eating, is a puzzle that never gets old.

There is also something about the rhythm of a cast — the loop unrolling above the water, the fly landing softly — that is genuinely meditative. The river demands full attention. There is no room for anything else.

Techniques

Dry fly fishing is the most visually satisfying — watching a fish rise to take a fly off the surface is unforgettable. But it requires a perfect presentation and an accurate read of what the fish are feeding on. Nymphing, fishing below the surface, is often more productive — especially in colder months when insect activity is low. Streamers, large wet flies fished actively, target predatory behaviour and can produce the largest fish.

Fly tying — making your own flies — adds another layer. Understanding which materials imitate which insects, how to tie a hackle, how to weight a nymph correctly. It is a craft in itself.

On patience A day on the river without a catch is still a good day. The environment, the focus required, the physical rhythm of casting — these have value independent of the result. The same mindset applies well to long IT projects: the process has value, not just the outcome.
